\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Recommendation Performance}
Table~\ref{tab:main_results} reports HR@10 and NDCG@10 under the
sampled evaluation protocol (99 random negatives, random tiebreak).

\input{tables/main_results}

\paragraph{Pipeline~A (Cleora + BGE-M3).}
Pipeline~A achieves the highest standalone HR@10 of \textbf{0.7626}
(NDCG@10 = 0.4867), a $+75.2\%$ improvement over the Content-KNN
baseline (0.4353).
By restricting the candidate space to items within the Cleora behavioral
co-purchase graph and ranking by BGE-M3 profile similarity, Pipeline~A
leverages both behavioral and semantic signals simultaneously.

\paragraph{DIF-SASRec (Pipeline~B).}
DIF-SASRec achieves HR@10 = 0.4836 (NDCG@10 = 0.3737), an $+11.1\%$
improvement over Content-KNN (0.4353), confirming that sequential
modeling captures preference dynamics that nearest-neighbor retrieval
misses.

\paragraph{Dual-pipeline system.}
The union of Pipeline~A and Pipeline~B top-10 lists achieves
\textbf{HR@10 = 0.7886} (NDCG@10 = 0.5409), the best overall result.
Table~\ref{tab:complementarity} breaks down the per-user hit patterns.
Pipeline~A rescues \textbf{59.0\%} of DIF-SASRec misses, while
DIF-SASRec rescues \textbf{11.0\%} of Pipeline~A misses.
The two pipelines serve genuinely different user--item patterns:
Pipeline~A excels on items with strong behavioral co-purchase signals
(within the Cleora 375k index), while DIF-SASRec provides complementary
sequential intent modeling for items Pipeline~A cannot reach.

\subsection{Robustness Under Stricter Evaluation}
\label{ssec:robustness}

Table~\ref{tab:robustness} repeats the comparison under a 999-negative
protocol (1\,000 candidates; random-chance baseline HR@10 = 0.01),
ten times harder than the standard setting.

\input{tables/robustness_results}

DIF-SASRec remains the strongest model at this harder setting
(HR@10 = 0.3142, NDCG@10 = 0.2209).
Content-KNN drops to 0.2122 HR@10.
Notably, DIF-SASRec's relative advantage over the random baseline
\emph{increases} from $4.8\times$ (99-negative) to $31\times$
(999-negative), indicating the model's discriminative ability is not
an artefact of easy negatives in the standard pool.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} reports both pipeline-level and component-level
ablations.

\input{tables/ablation}

\paragraph{Pipeline ablation.}
Pipeline~A alone (+57.6\% over Pipeline~B baseline) demonstrates that
behavioral co-purchase structure combined with BGE-M3 profile similarity
is a powerful retrieval signal in the books domain.
The full union system adds a further +3.4\% relative by incorporating
DIF-SASRec's complementary sequential modeling for items outside
Cleora's behavioral coverage.

\paragraph{Category auxiliary loss.}
Removing the auxiliary category classification head reduces DIF-SASRec
HR@10 by $1.8\%$, indicating that genre-level supervision provides a
useful inductive bias for distinguishing within-sequence preference drift.

\paragraph{Content veto.}
Disabling the cosine-similarity veto ($\tau=0.3$) in Pipeline~B reduces
HR@10 by $2.1\%$.
Without the veto, DIF-SASRec scores candidates that are semantically
unrelated to the user's latent interests, diluting recommendation precision.

\paragraph{Online adaptation.}
The largest single drop ($-7.9\%$ HR@10) comes from removing the
per-user fine-tuning step.
This confirms that the pretrained model alone under-fits individual
preference trajectories; the online gradient step is the primary driver
of personalisation beyond the pretrained prior.

\paragraph{Dual-stream attention.}
Replacing the decoupled content/category attention with a single merged
stream reduces HR@10 by $4.6\%$, validating that keeping textual and
categorical signals in separate attention heads prevents one modality
from dominating the other.

\subsection{Latency Analysis}
Table~\ref{tab:latency} reports the end-to-end latency breakdown measured
under two conditions on an NVIDIA RTX~5060~Ti: (i)~\emph{cold}, using 35
unique queries each executed exactly once so every observation is a
guaranteed LRU and FAISS page-cache miss, and (ii)~\emph{warm}, using
repeated queries after the server has been running long enough for the
HNSW index to be resident in OS page cache and the LRU caches to be
populated.

\input{tables/latency}

\paragraph{Cold path.}
On a unique query with no prior caching, P50 end-to-end latency is
\textbf{1\,127\,ms} for English and 1\,281\,ms for non-English input.
The dominant component is FAISS HNSW text search (P50~906\,ms), which
incurs OS page faults on the 12.6\,GB BGE-M3 HNSW index when graph nodes
are not resident in RAM.
BGE-M3 encoding contributes 40\,ms P50; NLLB translation adds 156\,ms
P50 only for non-English queries.
The flat-index baseline (4\,200\,ms) used prior to HNSW migration is still
$3.7\times$ slower than the cold HNSW path, confirming that HNSW delivers
a meaningful speedup independent of caching.

\paragraph{Warm path.}
Once the HNSW index is paged into OS RAM and LRU caches are populated,
P50 drops to \textbf{17\,ms} (internal) / \textbf{34\,ms} (E2E wall
clock) for English queries.
FAISS search falls to 2\,ms and both translation and encoding are served
from in-process LRU caches at under 1\,ms, so latency is bounded by
Tantivy BM25 ($\sim$6\,ms) and network/serialization overhead.

\paragraph{Production characterisation.}
In a deployed system, the server issues a warmup routine at startup
(pre-querying representative terms to page the HNSW index into RAM)
before accepting live traffic.
After warmup, unique new queries incur only the BGE-M3 encoding cost
($\sim$40\,ms P50) plus fast FAISS search ($\sim$2\,ms) once the index
is resident, yielding a realistic steady-state P50 of approximately
60--80\,ms for English queries.
Repeated or popular queries are served entirely from LRU caches at
$<$25\,ms P50.

\subsection{Concurrency and Memory}
The AgentPool of 8 independent DIF-SASRec instances sustains throughput
for 10+ simultaneous users with no observed weight corruption, validated
by stress-testing with 20 concurrent requests.
Each agent holds a pretrained weight snapshot in CPU RAM ($\sim$148\,MB)
for new-user cold-start initialization, with fine-tuned weights kept in
GPU memory only for the duration of the request.
Total pool VRAM footprint is 1.18\,GB, leaving headroom on a 24\,GB card
for the FAISS HNSW indices and encoder models.
