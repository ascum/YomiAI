\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

% Paragraph 1 — NBA context & multimedia gap
Next Best Action (NBA) systems identify the most appropriate action for each
customer at a given point in time, such as recommending products or
personalized offers.
Current deployments rely predominantly on \emph{structured} transaction data:
purchase logs, click histories, and demographic records.
Modern users increasingly generate \emph{unstructured and multimedia} data,
including natural language queries, images, and interaction sequences, which
provide crucial contextual signals for more accurate recommendations.

% Paragraph 2 — Domain narrowing & interaction-content gap
Book discovery is a representative testbed for this challenge.
Users who know what they want issue free-text or image-based queries
(active search), while passive browsers benefit from proactive suggestions
derived from their interaction history (passive recommendation).
Most systems address only one mode, leaving an \emph{interaction-content gap}:
behavioral co-purchase signals are decoupled from semantic content understanding.
Collaborative filtering excels at crowd wisdom but suffers from cold-start
and ignores multimodal content; sequential models~\cite{kang2018self} capture
preference evolution but discard visual signals; content-based encoders retrieve
relevant items but lack behavioral graph structure.

% Paragraph 3 — Our approach & contributions
To address these challenges, we propose a \textbf{Dual-Mode Multimodal NBA Recommendation System} that jointly models active search and passive personalization, integrating heterogeneous signals from text, images, and user behavior.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Mode~1 (Active Search):}  a multilingual query-driven
        retrieval component that combines textual and visual representation via Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF), with optional BM25 keyword boosting and multi-layer LRU caching.
  \item \textbf{Mode~2 (Passive Recommendation):} two complementary pipelines, consisting of (a) a graph-based component capturing behavioral similarity, and (b) a sequential model capturing evolving user preferences.
  \item A \textbf{shared user profile} continuously updated after every
        interaction, bridging both modes  temporal weighting and
        enabling online adaptation of the sequential model.
\end{itemize}
